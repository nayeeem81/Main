The z-index property controls the three-dimensional stacking order of elements on a web page, determining which elements sit on top of others along the virtual "Z-axis" (pointing toward the viewer).

Here is exactly how these specific numbers behave in your code:

The Rule of Higher Numbers

Larger numbers sit on top: An element with a higher z-index value will always stack in front of an element with a lower value.

The Layering: In your layout, the .layout-selector-panel (z-index: 2000) will float directly on top of the .navbar (z-index: 1000) if they ever overlap.

The Background: Both elements will sit completely ahead of any standard webpage content that uses default or lower z-index values.

Why 1000 and 2000 Are UsedSafety Buffers: Web developers use large increments like 1000 and 2000 to leave "room" in between layers.Future Proofing: If you later need to insert a dropdown menu inside the navbar, you can safely give it a z-index of 1500. It will sit above the navbar, but still slide underneath the selector panel.

The Hidden Rule: Stacking ContextPositioning Required: 

The z-index property only works on elements that have an explicit position value set (like absolute, relative, fixed, or sticky).

Your Code: Your .layout-selector-panel has position: absolute;, so its z-index: 2000 works perfectly. 

However, for your .navbar's z-index: 1000 to work, you must also add a position rule (such as position: relative; or position: fixed;) to it.
